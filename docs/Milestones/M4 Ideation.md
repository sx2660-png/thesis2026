# M4 Ideation: Trading Brain

## Project Background and Design Challenge

Trading Brain is intended for the Guangdong day-ahead electricity trading context. It is designed to help traders bring together policy documents, market information, price forecasts, grid constraints, and strategy outputs before submitting a declaration. My M3 research suggested that the main difficulty is not simply a lack of information. Instead, important information is spread across different sources, and those sources vary in timing, relevance, and reliability. Under a strict submission deadline, a trader has to decide whether a warning deserves attention, which trading periods may be affected, and whether the current automated strategy should continue.

This led me to a more focused design challenge: how can a system turn scattered, time-sensitive, and sometimes incomplete information into something that a trader can inspect and act on? The system should make it possible to understand why a warning appeared, which evidence supports it, and what remains uncertain. It should help the trader decide whether to continue, recalculate, pause, or ask another person to review the situation. Human judgment is therefore not an extra step at the end of the process. It is part of the process itself.

## From Research to Ideas

The research led me to four main concerns. First, information should be organised around the trading decision rather than around separate agents or data sources. Second, the timing of information needs to be clear. Evidence available before the decision deadline should be distinguished from information published later or information with an uncertain publication time. Third, a warning should not be reduced to a single risk score. It should explain what triggered it, which periods may be affected, and what the trader might do next. Finally, the trader should be able to question, change, or reject a recommendation, with the decision saved for later review.

These concerns became the main design principles for the next stage. The first screen should answer whether the trader needs to intervene. Supporting evidence should be more visible than an unexplained confidence score. The strength of the evidence should affect the strength of the recommendation. High-risk actions should have a clear human confirmation or pause step. The system should also fit into the existing trading workflow rather than asking traders to move to a completely separate process. These principles are informed by human-centred design, cognitive load and progressive disclosure, information foraging, and research on calibrated trust in human-AI interaction. Together, they suggest that the value of the system is not in showing more information, but in helping the user find the information that matters for the current decision.

To avoid choosing a dashboard too early, I used a simple brainwriting process and considered several ways of organising the problem. The first idea was an Alert-First Decision Console, centred on a warning card showing the risk, reason, affected periods, and suggested action. This would respond well to time pressure, but it could oversimplify a complex situation. The second idea was a Decision Timeline, showing how a news item or policy rule was found, checked, and eventually connected to a warning. This could make the system's reasoning more transparent, although it might not be the most efficient first view. The third idea was an Evidence Triage Inbox, where evidence could be grouped as verified, pending review, background information, or rejected. This would support source checking, but it might also shift too much reading work to the trader.

The fourth idea was a Market Scenario Cockpit, combining price curves, load forecasts, grid constraints, and possible market situations. This could support deeper strategy analysis, but it would depend heavily on reliable data and forecasts. The fifth idea was a Daily Trading Brief, a short summary generated before the declaration deadline. This would be useful for routine review, but less useful when a trader needs to investigate the reason behind a warning. The sixth idea was a Feedback and Model Operations Lab for reviewing false positives, missed warnings, source performance, and trader comments. This could support long-term improvement, but it should not become the main interface for daily trading decisions.

These ideas are not necessarily separate products. The most useful combination seems to be an alert console as the entry point, a timeline to explain how a warning was produced, an evidence view to show the status and quality of sources, and annotation and history features for later review. The scenario cockpit and daily brief can remain possible future extensions because they would expand the scope of the M5 prototype and require more complete market data.

## Provisional Direction and Connection to M5

The direction I will take into M5 is an alert-first human review interface. It will not execute trades on behalf of the user. Instead, it will organise the review around a small set of practical questions: What is happening now? Do I need to look into it? What triggered the warning? Which trading periods may be affected? What can I do next? The default view would show the review status, a short risk summary, the affected periods, and the suggested action. The trader could then expand the view to inspect the evidence sources, timing information, trigger chain, and previous decisions. This structure aims to balance the need for speed with the need for transparency.

This direction also connects to the current prototype. Today’s Warning can represent the alert and human review step. Transaction Flow can show the path from news search and policy analysis to risk checking. Key Evidence can present the source, publication time, and evidence status. History Replay and Annotation can support review of previous decisions and trader feedback. At this stage, the prototype should be understood as an early design for testing these interactions. It does not claim that the system can make reliable trading decisions automatically.

My initial hypothesis is that a dashboard which presents review status and a possible next action first, while keeping evidence and technical details available through progressive disclosure, will support faster and better-calibrated judgments than an interface that shows raw search results, scattered market signals, or a single model score. This hypothesis still needs to be developed and tested through the M5 prototype. The outcome of M4 is therefore not a final dashboard specification. It is a provisional direction selected from several ways of organising the problem: a human review process that turns time-sensitive and incomplete market information into a decision that can be inspected, questioned, and recorded.
